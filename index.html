1887
The San Francisco Examiner
William Randolph Hearst takes over the Examiner from his father, George Hearst, transforming it into a sensational, circulation-driven daily.
Milestone
1895
New York Morning Journal Acquired
Enters the fierce New York newspaper market, igniting the legendary circulation war with Joseph Pulitzer's World.
Acquisition
1896
"Yellow Journalism" Era Begins
The Journal-World rivalry defines a new era of sensational, mass-market journalism. The "Yellow Kid" comic gives the era its name.
Milestone
1903
Los Angeles Examiner Founded
Expansion to the West Coast. By 1910, the chain includes papers in Chicago, Boston, and Atlanta.
Expansion
1911
King Features Syndicate
Established to distribute comics, columns, and editorial content. Eventually becomes the world's largest syndicate, home to Blondie, Beetle Bailey, and Popeye.
Launch
1919
Magazine Empire Begins
Acquires a collection of magazines and enters book publishing. By the 1920s, the portfolio includes Cosmopolitan, Harper's Bazaar, and Good Housekeeping.
Expansion

1926
Peak Newspaper Empire
Operates 28 newspapers in 18 cities — the largest newspaper chain in America. Reaches a combined daily circulation of nearly 6 million.
Milestone
1927
Hearst Metrotone News
Enters film and newsreel production. Hearst newsreels become a fixture in American movie theaters throughout the 1930s and '40s.
Launch
1935
San Simeon & Cultural Zenith
Hearst Castle nears completion. WR Hearst's art collection is among the world's largest. His media empire touches 30 million Americans daily.
Milestone
1937
Financial Crisis & Reorganization
Overextension forces a massive sell-off. The empire consolidates under professional management, emerging leaner but solvent.
Milestone
1941
Citizen Kane Released
Orson Welles' thinly-veiled portrait of WR Hearst becomes one of cinema's greatest films — and a lifelong irritant to its subject.
Milestone

1951
William Randolph Hearst Dies
The founder passes at 88. Control transfers to a trust overseen by 13 trustees — 5 family, 8 non-family — a structure that endures today.
Milestone
1953
Richard E. Berlin Era
Berlin becomes CEO, steering the company through the collapse of afternoon newspapers. Focus shifts toward magazines and broadcasting.
Milestone
1966
Television Expansion
Hearst begins aggressively acquiring television stations, building what becomes a major network of ABC, NBC, and CBS affiliates.
Expansion
1974
Patty Hearst & the SLA
The kidnapping of William Randolph Hearst III's daughter becomes one of the most sensational news stories of the decade.
Milestone
1979
Frank Bennack Jr. Becomes CEO
Bennack begins a transformative 23-year tenure, pivoting the company from legacy print toward cable television and business media.
Milestone
